Law 7 of power teaches that one should get others to do the work while taking the credit for the results. This principle is rooted in efficiency, influence, and strategic positioning. It does not simply mean exploiting people without thought, but rather understanding how to organize effort, leverage the strengths of others, and present outcomes in a way that elevates your status. Power grows when you can achieve more than what your individual capacity allows, and this is often done through the work, ideas, and energy of others. At the center of this law is the concept of leverage. No individual, regardless of talent or intelligence, can master everything or accomplish large goals alone. Time, energy, and knowledge are limited resources. By involving others, you multiply your reach. You tap into different skills, experiences, and perspectives that you may not possess. This allows you to produce results that appear impressive and comprehensive, even if your direct contribution was only a part of the whole. Another key idea is the use of accumulated knowledge. Throughout history, progress has been built upon the ideas and discoveries of others. Innovators, leaders, and creators often stand on the shoulders of those who came before them. By studying, adapting, and applying existing knowledge, you can move forward faster than if you attempted to start from nothing. This is not theft of ideas, but strategic use of available resources. The important part is how you combine, refine, and present these elements to create something that appears unified and original. This law also highlights the importance of delegation. When you assign tasks to others, you free yourself to focus on higher level thinking, planning, and decision making. Delegation allows you to operate at a strategic level rather than being consumed by operational details. It also creates an image of authority, as those who direct work are often perceived as more powerful than those who execute it. However effective delegation requires judgment. You must choose the right people, provide clear direction, and ensure that the outcome aligns with your vision. There is also a psychological dimension to this law. People often associate results with the person who presents them, not necessarily the one who created them. Visibility plays a role here. If you position yourself as the face of a project, people will naturally connect the success to you. This is why presentation, communication, and timing are critical. By controlling how results are revealed, you shape perception and secure recognition. At the same time, this law requires subtlety. Taking credit too aggressively or obviously can create resentment. Those who feel used or unrecognized may withdraw support or even work against you. Therefore it is important to balance recognition and control. Sometimes acknowledging contributions, even in small ways, can maintain loyalty while still preserving your central position. The goal is to appear as the leader or organizer, not as someone who disregards others completely. Another aspect of this law is the ability to identify talent. Power increases when you surround yourself with capable individuals. Their strengths become extensions of your own. By understanding what each person can do best, you can assign roles that maximize efficiency and effectiveness. This creates a system where each part contributes to a larger outcome, and you remain at the center of coordination. This principle also applies in competitive environments. When you can direct others to work toward your goals, you reduce the burden on yourself while increasing output. Meanwhile competitors who rely only on their own efforts may struggle to keep up. This creates an advantage that compounds over time. The more effectively you leverage others, the greater your capacity becomes. However there is an ethical consideration in how this law is applied. There is a difference between leadership and exploitation. Leadership involves guiding, organizing, and creating opportunities for others while achieving shared goals. Exploitation ignores fairness and can damage trust. In many modern contexts, sustainable success depends on maintaining relationships and reputation. Therefore applying this law with awareness and respect can lead to long term influence, while misuse can lead to isolation. Another layer of this law involves learning from others mistakes and successes. By observing what others have done, you avoid unnecessary trial and error. This saves time and reduces risk. It allows you to refine strategies before applying them. In this sense, you are not only using others work directly but also indirectly through knowledge and experience. In modern settings, this law is visible in many forms. Leaders in organizations rely on teams to execute projects. Entrepreneurs build businesses by combining the efforts of employees, partners, and systems. Content creators collaborate, adapt trends, and build upon existing ideas. In each case, the individual who coordinates and presents the outcome often receives the recognition, even though many contributed to the result. This law also connects to the idea of focus. By offloading certain tasks, you protect your time and attention for what matters most. This can be vision, strategy, or personal development. Over time this focus leads to better decisions and stronger positioning. It allows you to operate at a level where your impact is greater than your direct effort. At its core, Law 7 teaches that power is not just about what you do, but about how effectively you use what is available around you. It is about turning collective effort into individual advantage, and turning scattered resources into organized results. It requires awareness of people, systems, and perception. When applied skillfully, it allows you to achieve outcomes that would be impossible alone, while maintaining a position of authority and recognition. In essence, this law is about working smarter rather than harder. It is about understanding that influence grows when you can direct energy, not just expend it. By mastering the ability to harness the work and ideas of others, you expand your reach, strengthen your presence, and move closer to positions of lasting power and control.
