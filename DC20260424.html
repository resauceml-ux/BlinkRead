The ninth book teaches that injustice is not only wrong toward other people but also impious toward the universe, because all rational beings are made to support one another and not to harm one another, therefore when a person acts unjustly he breaks the natural order and becomes an enemy of the common good, and this also applies to lying, because truth is rooted in nature itself, so anyone who lies knowingly separates himself from what is true and harms his own reason, while even unintentional error shows a failure to live in harmony with nature. Pleasure and pain are also discussed, as people often chase pleasure as if it were good and avoid pain as if it were evil, yet both are actually indifferent in the grand order of the universe, and by treating them as absolute good or evil, people end up blaming nature itself, which leads to further confusion and injustice, and this attachment to pleasure often causes people to act wrongly, proving that their judgment is flawed. It is said that it would be ideal to live free from falsehood and pride, but if that is not possible, one should at least grow tired of such a life and seek to leave it behind, since the corruption of the mind is far worse than any physical sickness, and a wise person should recognize this and strive to escape mental corruption above all else. Death is presented as something natural and not to be feared, just like growth and aging, it is simply another stage in the cycle of life, therefore it should be accepted calmly and without resistance, and if one seeks motivation to accept death, it helps to reflect on the nature of the body and the imperfections of society, not with bitterness but with clarity, understanding that death frees us from a world where people often fail to live according to reason. The text emphasizes that wrongdoing harms the person who commits it, not the one who receives it, and that even neglecting to do what is right can be a form of injustice, therefore one must focus on maintaining correct judgment, kind actions, and acceptance of whatever happens as part of divine order. The importance of controlling the mind is repeated, as one must remove false impressions, restrain desires, and keep the mind free and stable, because disturbances come from within rather than from external events, and peace can only be achieved by correcting one’s own thoughts. All rational beings share a common nature and are meant to be united, just as elements like earth and air naturally move toward each other, yet humans often forget this and separate themselves through selfishness and conflict, which goes against their true nature, still nature eventually brings everything back into unity, showing that harmony is inevitable. Each being has its purpose, and for humans, it is to act with reason and contribute to the common good, therefore every action should aim at benefiting others, and anything that does not serve this purpose is disorderly and harmful to the unity of society. When dealing with others, one should either teach them if possible or bear with them patiently, remembering that even the gods treat people with kindness despite their faults, and that human beings are naturally connected and should support one another rather than react with anger or frustration. Many troubles arise from internal opinions rather than external events, and by removing these opinions, one can free oneself from unnecessary suffering, since things themselves do not disturb us, but our judgments about them do. Everything in the world is temporary and constantly changing, and nothing lasts for long, therefore it is foolish to become attached to reputation, possessions, or even life itself, as all things eventually fade, and understanding this helps to reduce fear and attachment. The text also advises examining the nature of things, understanding their causes, and recognizing their limits in time, which helps in seeing them clearly without exaggeration, and this clarity leads to better judgment and less emotional disturbance. It is important to remember that the faults of others belong to them, not to us, and therefore should not trouble us, instead we should focus on our own actions and maintain our own integrity, since true harm only occurs when our own mind becomes corrupted. Life is described as a series of changes and endings, from childhood to old age, and none of these stages should cause distress, therefore the final ending of life should also be seen as natural and not something to fear. One should always return to three sources of guidance, one’s own reason to ensure justice, the nature of the universe to remember one’s place within it, and the understanding of others to recognize their limitations and act with compassion. Human actions should always aim toward the common good, as humans are social beings, and any action that separates us from this purpose is harmful and disruptive, like a person who breaks away from a united group. Complaints and dissatisfaction are unnecessary, as they come from misunderstanding the nature of life, and by accepting things as they are and focusing on fulfilling one’s role, one can find peace and stability. The fleeting nature of life is emphasized again, as even those who seek fame will soon be forgotten, and both the observer and the observed will vanish, making external validation meaningless. It is within our power to remove many of the things that trouble us, as they are based on perception rather than reality, and by adjusting our thoughts, we create space for calm and clarity. Everything in the universe follows either divine order or natural consequence, and in either case, there is no reason to complain, because we can still govern our own actions and thoughts, which is what truly matters. The text concludes by encouraging simplicity, modesty, and genuine virtue, rather than imitation of others or pursuit of recognition, reminding us that true philosophy lies in living according to nature, doing good for others, and maintaining inner peace regardless of external circumstances.
